## **Spreads That Don't Let You Hide**

*|FNAME|*, most people use spreads to organize information.

You use them to excavate.

Past, present, future — that's the surface structure. But underneath every spread lives another layer: what's conscious versus what's buried, what's being shown versus what's being hidden, what the querent wants to hear versus what they need to face.

You already read for what's underneath. 

And as a performing aerialist, I can relate… 

I've built dozens of routines. Each one forced me to face the same uncomfortable truth: there is no "right" way to begin.

Sometimes the song would arrive first, and the movement would emerge from the music.

Sometimes a sequence would demand to be choreographed, and only later would the perfect song reveal itself.

Rarely, a costume piece would spark the entire concept—then I'd pull from my library of techniques and hunt down the song that fit.

Three completely different pathways. Same destination.

All of them worked. None of them were "correct."

That uncertainty used to paralyze me. I'd stand there waiting for the "right" starting point to announce itself, as if there was some proper order I was supposed to follow.

There wasn't.

The routine didn't care whether I started with music or movement or costume. It only cared that I was willing to begin somewhere and follow what emerged.

### **Spreads Work the Same Way**

Three cards? Quick reconnaissance. What's actually here right now, stripped of story?

Celtic Cross? A detailed excavation mapping multiple layers. What's underneath what you think you already know?

One card? Complete stillness. What reveals itself when you stop trying to figure it out?

There's no "wrong" spread. Just different ways of descending into what you're asking.

But here's what people actually struggle with: they want permission. They want someone to tell them the "right" way to lay cards so they can't mess it up.

That's not how this works.

The cards don't care if you use three or ten. They care whether you're willing to face what they show you.

Just like aerial routines, the structure is less important than your willingness to start somewhere and trust what wants to emerge—even when you have no idea if you're "doing it right."

You're not.

And that's exactly the point.

### **How Standard Spreads Enable Avoidance**

"Outcome" positions let you fish for the answer you want. You pull cards until you get the version that says it'll work out. The spread becomes fortune-telling instead of truth-telling.

"Advice" positions let you ignore what you already know. The cards tell you what to do—conveniently—instead of showing you what you're avoiding seeing.

"What's blocking you?" lets you externalize. It's always something outside you. The spread confirms: it's not your fault, something's blocking you. Never: you're the one refusing to move.

Vague position labels create wiggle room for comfortable interpretations. "Energy around this situation" can mean anything you need it to mean.

The structure itself enables the lie.

**Architecture That Forces Honesty**

Here's a spread that closes the escape routes:

**Position 1: What I'm pretending not to know** Not "what should I know" or "hidden influences"—what you already see but won't admit.

**Position 2: What this pretending is costing me** Actual cost. Not potential future consequence—what it's costing right now to maintain this lie.

**Position 3: What happens if I keep avoiding this** Not "what might happen if"—what WILL happen when you continue this pattern.

**Position 4: What becomes possible if I face it** Not outcome—possibility. What opens when you stop hiding.

**Position 5: The first honest action** Not advice. Action. The concrete thing you do next if you're serious about truth.

No "outcome" to fish for. No "advice" to rationalize away. No externalization of the problem.

Just: Here's what's true. Here's what it costs. Here's what happens if you don't change. Here's what's possible if you do. Here's the next step.

### **Designing Your Own Truth-Telling Spreads**

Principles:

**Start with the question underneath the question.** Not "Will this work out?" but "What am I actually afraid of here?" Not "What should I do?" but "What am I avoiding doing?"

**Build positions that close escape routes.** No vague labels. No "energy" positions. Specific, direct questions that require honest answers.

**Include at least one "what am I avoiding?" position.** Force the spread to reveal the avoidance mechanism, not just the surface situation.

**End with action, not validation.** The last card isn't "how this resolves"—it's "what you do next." Make the reading demand something from you.

**Make it impossible to get a comfortable reading.** If you can pull this spread and walk away feeling good without changing anything, the spread isn't built right.

**Examples of Honest Position Names:**
- 

What I'm refusing to see
- 

Where I'm lying to myself
- 

What this pattern is protecting me from feeling
- 

What I know I need to do but won't
- 

What I'm using this situation to avoid
- 

The truth I don't want to accept
- 

What I'm getting from staying stuck

These don't allow comfortable interpretations. They demand honesty.

### **This Is Advanced Work**

Not every reading needs this level of confrontation. Sometimes you need a gentle check-in. Sometimes a simple three-card past-present-future serves.

But when you're stuck—when you've been asking the same question for months, when you keep pulling cards hoping for a different answer, when you know you're avoiding something but can't see what—this is the architecture you need.

Spreads that don't let you hide.
