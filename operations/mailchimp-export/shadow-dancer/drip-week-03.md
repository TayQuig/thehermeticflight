## **Depth Is Your Tarot Superpower**

*|FNAME|*, we've named the gift. We've faced the shadow.

Now let's talk about what your *|TYPE_ADJ|* mind actually does with 78 cards.

Tarot has a reputation for light-and-love platitudes. You've probably noticed that doesn't sit right with you.

Good. Because the cards weren't designed for comfort. They were designed for truth.

Here's what nobody tells seekers: Your questions aren't preparation for the real work. They ARE the work.

But I had to learn that through blood and ice packs.

When I started training aerial silks, my coach was a phenomenal performer. And a terrible teacher. 

She didn't offer information. 

I didn't ask for it.

Perfect recipe for disaster.

For six weeks, I trained four days a week on partially subluxed shoulders. My shoulders were literally coming out of their sockets, and I just... kept going.

Not because I was brave. Because I was avoiding the truth my body was screaming.

I loved aerial more than I loved listening to what was actually happening in my body. So I iced. I trained. I iced again. Twice-weekly chiropractor visits where he'd say, "You should really stop doing this."

I didn't stop.

My heart couldn't handle giving up the thing I loved. So I chose to ignore what was destroying me instead.

That's the part we don't talk about—how easily we turn passion into self-harm. How we'll train through injury, ignore professional advice, and call it dedication.

It took a physical therapist who specialized in aerialists to finally break through my denial.

"You're not engaging your shoulders and upper back. They're just hanging there."

I'd been doing it exactly how I was taught. Which meant I was doing it wrong, and nobody—including me—had been willing to face that.

The solution was simple once I stopped avoiding it: engage the muscles. Keep the joint in socket. That's it.

Months of PT. Shoulders healing. Form improving. No more sleeping with ice packs.

But here's the deeper truth: I had to be willing to ask the questions I'd been too afraid to voice. "Is this supposed to hurt?" "What am I doing wrong?" "Why is my body breaking?"

Those questions didn't make me weak. My refusal to ask them almost ended my practice entirely.

Curiosity isn't just about seeking new knowledge. It's about being brave enough to question what you think you already know.

Even when the answer reveals you've been hurting yourself all along.

That's how this connects to tarot: real seeking means confronting what you've been avoiding.

### **Why Depth-Seekers Excel at Tarot**

You're already comfortable with shadow—and half the deck lives there.

The Tower doesn't scare you because you understand that some structures need to fall. The Death card doesn't send you spiraling because you know that endings make space for what's next. The Devil doesn't represent evil to you—it shows where you're bound and pretending you're free.

You don't need cards to be nice. You need them to be true.

Most readers soften the difficult cards. They reframe the Ten of Swords as "this is ending" instead of "this is actual betrayal." They read the Five of Cups as "hidden blessings" instead of "genuine loss that needs to be felt."

You don't do that. You spot the hidden patterns others miss because you're willing to look underneath the comfortable interpretation.

When you pull the Eight of Swords, you don't hear "you're blocking yourself." You see the actual bindings—the real ways you're constrained, the true limitations you're working within.

Uncomfortable truths don't scare you away from accurate readings. They make the reading more useful.

### **How Your Lens Transforms Tarot Practice**

Where others see "bad cards," you see necessary information.

You read the Devil and see liberation through honest acknowledgment of where you're actually bound—not "release" through denial, but freedom through facing what's true.

You pull the Tower and recognize that some things need to fall. Not everything requires saving. Sometimes the collapse IS the healing.

The Death card doesn't frighten you—stagnation does. You'd rather face an ending than pretend something's still alive when it's been dead for months.

The Moon shows you're in confusion and you don't immediately try to find clarity. You trust that some paths require walking in the dark first.

This is what makes you good at tarot: you don't need the cards to protect you from reality. You need them to show you what's actually there.

### **The Depth Advantage**

Transformation requires seeing what's actually there—not what you wish were there, not the comfortable story, the truth.

Tarot rewards the willingness to look directly at shadow without flinching.

Your comfort with darkness lets the cards speak honestly. They don't have to sugarcoat. They don't have to reassure. They can show you the Tower falling and trust that you can handle that information.

This isn't morbidity. This is the only way real change happens.

You don't transform what you won't acknowledge. Tarot puts everything on the table—light and shadow, growth and decay, building and collapse. You're one of the few who can look at all of it without needing to make the difficult parts mean something comfortable.

That's your advantage.

Everyone has "scary" cards they're afraid to pull. The Tower. Death. The Devil. Ten of Swords. Eight of Swords.

But you probably have one that everyone else fears that makes perfect sense to you. One that doesn't scare you because you understand what it's actually showing.

What is it?
