## **How Curiosity Unlocks Tarot**

*|FNAME|*, we've talked about your gifts. We've talked about your shadows.

Now let's talk about what happens when curiosity meets tarot.

Here's the thing nobody tells seekers:

Your questions aren't preparation for practice. They ARE the practice. 

The questions themselves; the wondering, the what-if, the turning cards over in your mind; that IS the work.

But first—a story about the time incomplete information nearly destroyed my shoulders.

I remember when I first started training aerial under a coach who was an incredible performer, but weirdly enough, they hated teaching.

I was so excited about learning new things that I forgot to ask questions.

I had no idea certain moves weren’t supposed to hurt.

I accidentally trained aerial silk, 4 days a week, on partially subluxed shoulders for 6 entire weeks. 

All because I didn’t ask questions.

My shoulders kept coming out of socket, but I loved aerial so much that I dealt with the pain and kept on training and icing like a mad woman. 

I would visit my insanely patient chiropractor twice a week and he would say something along the lines of “you should really consider stopping aerial because your shoulders keep coming out” or “take a break”.

But my heart couldn’t handle it. 

It was only until a beautiful PT, that specializes in aerialists, walked into my life, did I finally understand my issue. 

I wasn’t fully engaging my shoulders and upper back.

My shoulders were just hanging!

I thought it was proper form because I was doing it how I had been taught!

I didn’t ask my coach questions, and she wasn’t volunteering any body mechanics information.

I worked with my PT for months and discovered that, so long as I engaged my shoulders and upper back when I hung from the silks, my shoulders remained in their sockets. 

It was LIFE CHANGING.

My chiropractor was happy, my shoulders began to heal and strengthen, and my form rapidly improved.

Best of all? I didn’t have to go to sleep with ice packs on my shoulders every night anymore.

My lessons sowed more curiosity and I discovered that, constantly seeking not only kept my body safe, but it made me a better aerialist and instructor.

And how does this relate to tarot?

It’s all about embracing that curiosity.

### **Why Tarot Loves Curious Minds**

### 

The cards are designed to generate questions.

Not provide certainty.

Think about it: each card contains multiple meanings. 

The Tower can be catastrophic collapse or necessary destruction. The Three of Swords can be heartbreak or the clarity that cuts through illusion. Death can be literal ending or total transformation.

The "right" interpretation? It's the one you discover through being open, not the one you memorize from a book.

Curiosity helps you find which meaning applies now. For this person. In this context. With these surrounding cards.

Spreads are invitations to explore relationships between cards. Not fixed formulas producing guaranteed answers, but frameworks for discovery. 

"What happens when these three cards appear together? What story wants to emerge?"

The practice rewards minds that ask "what else could this mean?" instead of stopping at the first answer.

That's you.

### **How Your Curiosity Serves Your Practice**

### 

You notice details others skip because you haven't decided what matters yet.

Someone who "knows" what the Four of Cups means might miss the figure's specific body language, the exact color of the sky, the way the offered cup is positioned differently from the three on the ground. Much like someone who knows aerial moves may miss a cool combination because your curiosity drives you to look for different pathways. 

You see those details. Because you're curious. Because you haven't closed the door to what they might reveal.

Your willingness to try different interpretations reveals layers of meaning. "What if it means this? Or what if it means that? What if both are true?"

You ask "what else could this mean?" when others stop at the first answer. And that questioning, that refusal to settle too quickly, that's not you being indecisive.

 That's you being thorough.

Your comfort with ambiguity matches tarot's actual nature. 

Nothing is ever just one thing. The cards hold contradiction, complexity, nuance. You're comfortable there.

Others find it destabilizing.

### **Curiosity in Action: What This Looks Like**

### 

Pulling a card and spending five minutes noticing details before looking up the meaning. Not because you don't trust the guidebook, but because you want to see what YOU see first. Like how many ways can you enter a belay?

Asking "why did these three cards show up together?" instead of reading them as three separate, unrelated meanings. Exploring the relationship, the conversation, the tension or harmony between them. 

This is what makes the cards sing.

Experimenting with different spreads for the same question to see what shifts. Past-present-future might reveal one thing. 

A decision spread might reveal something else. 

The question stays the same; the angle changes. What do you discover through different approaches? Different sequencing? 

Rearranging?

Following threads of meaning even when they lead somewhere unexpected. Even when they contradict what you thought you knew. Even when they don't fit the narrative you wanted. 

You find yourself some where completely exciting.

Trusting "I wonder if..." as a valid form of interpretation. As real and useful as "the book says" or "everyone knows."

Your wondering IS the reading. Not the prelude to it.

### **Try This:**

Pull a single card right now. Before you look up anything, before you check the guidebook, before you let any external authority tell you what you're seeing—spend two minutes just noticing.

Colors. Figures. Objects. Mood. Direction. What draws your eye? What feels significant? What are you curious about?

I'd love to see what you noticed, not what it "means," just what caught your eye.

What tugged at your attention? What made you wonder? Did you choose a different combination than typical?? Share it with me.

That's where interpretation lives. In your direct perception. In your willingness to see without needing the "correct" answer. In playing with curiosity.

Trust your eyes.
