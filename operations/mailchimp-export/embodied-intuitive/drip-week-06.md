## **Different Maps for Different Questions**

*|FNAME|*, you've explored the territories. You've met the guides.

Now: which path do you take?

Here's what your *|TYPE_ADJ|* mind will love—there's no single right answer.

Every spread is a different route through the same terrain.

As an aerialist, I have had the opportunity to perform. 

It has been incredible for my development and has helped me expand my abilities in multiple disciplines.

Sometimes the song would inspire the move, the sequence.

Sometimes the move would inspire the sequence and the song would later find me.

And rarely, but sometimes, a costume piece inspired the concept where I would pull from my library of techniques and then find the perfect song.

All of these pathways are so different, yet all led me to the same point.

A combination of information that inspired a story.

Every spread is a different path through the same territory.

Three cards? 

A quick reconnaissance; what's right here, right now? 

Celtic Cross? 

A detailed expedition mapping multiple layers. 

One card? 

A moment of standing completely still and noticing what's present.

There's no "wrong" spread. Just different ways of exploring what you're asking. Much like creating an aerial routine on your favorite apparatus.

### **Spreads as Invitations to Wander**

### 

What if a spread isn't a rule you must follow, but a suggested route through terrain you're exploring?

Think of it like trails in a forest, or pathways through aerial silk. 

The marked trail gets you somewhere specific; it's been walked before, it has a destination, it offers a particular view. 

The pathway has been discovered and blocked. But you're allowed to step off the common path. 

To follow something interesting. 

To discover what's not on any map. 

Discover your own unique sequence.

Different spreads reveal different aspects of the same question. 

Ask about a relationship using a simple three-card read and you see one view.

Ask using a relationship-specific spread and suddenly you're noticing dynamics you missed the first time. Much like reorganizing combinations in a routine, leading you to find something more exciting. 

The structure supports your exploration. It doesn't constrain it.

What if you could trust both the path and your freedom to wander from it?

### **Questions and the Paths They Suggest**

### 

Have you noticed that some questions feel... small? Contained? While others sprawl in every direction?

**Simple questions might call for simple paths:** "What energy surrounds this situation right now?"  Maybe 1-3 cards. Quick. Direct. Immediate insight into what's present.

**Process questions might invite journey-shaped paths:** "How do I move through this transition?"  Linear spreads showing progression. 

Beginning, middle, outcome. Or challenge, action, resolution. The structure mirrors the journey you're asking about.

**Complex questions might want territory maps:** "What are all the factors at play in this decision?"  

Celtic Cross or larger spreads. 

Multiple angles. 

Hidden influences. 

What you hope for and what you fear. 

What's visible and what's not.

**Wide-open questions might prefer wandering paths:** "What do I need to see right now?"  Intuitive pulls without preset positions. 

Let the cards speak to each other without predetermined roles. See what wants to emerge.

What if you let the question itself suggest the route? But stayed flexible enough to change course if needed?

Sometimes you plan a three-card read and realize halfway through you need more cards. Sometimes you lay out ten cards and realize the first three told you everything. Both are valid.

### **What You Might Notice in Any Spread**

Regardless of which path you choose, I wonder what you'd discover if you looked for these things:

**Which territories are appearing?** 

Count the suits if you're curious. Are you seeing mostly Cups; all emotion and connection? Mostly Pentacles; grounded in material concerns? 

The concentration of suits might tell you where your question actually lives, even if you thought you were asking about something else.

**Which stages of the journey show up?** Look at the numbers and court cards. Are you seeing a lot of beginnings (Aces, Pages)? Or completion energy (Tens, Kings)? 

Or are you somewhere in the messy middle (Fives, Sixes, Knights)? What does that suggest about where you are in this story?

**What relationships exist between cards?** Are cards touching each other? Facing toward or away from each other? 

Do you see visual echoes; similar colors, similar body language, similar energy? What conversation seems to be happening between them?

**What story wants to emerge?** Not "what does position 1 mean, position 2 mean, position 3 mean" as separate, isolated facts. But, what's the narrative trying to form? What wants to be said through these cards together?

Your curiosity about the connections is often more revealing than any predetermined position meaning.

### **Creating Your Own Routes**

Once you feel comfortable with the territories (suits) and the journey stages (numbers, court cards), what if you designed your own spreads?

Not because the traditional ones don't work. But because sometimes your question needs a path that doesn't exist yet.

Want to explore past/present/future? Three cards in a line, simple, classic, effective.

Want to explore a decision between two options? What if you laid out parallel paths? "If I choose option A" (three cards). "If I choose option B" (three cards). See what each path reveals.

Want to explore something that feels complex and multifaceted? 

Map it however makes sense to you. 

"What I see clearly / What I'm not seeing / What I'm afraid of / What wants to emerge"? 

Four positions you just created because they matched your wondering.

The structure serves your exploration. You're not serving the structure.

What would you design if you trusted that your question already knows what it needs?
